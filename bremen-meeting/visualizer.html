Data

  • We used secondary data from Chilean Longitudinal Social Study (ELSOC)

  • It is a panel survey that runs every year from 2016 to 2023, making it the only study of this kind in Chile and Latin America

  • For the visualization, the analysis includes participants who took part in at least three waves of the study, totaling 3,666 individuals.

We used secondary data from the Chilean Longitudinal Social Study, a representative survey of Chile’s urban population that runs every year from 2016 (two thousand sixteen) to 2023 (two thousand twenty-three), making it a unique source of information of its kind in Chile and Latin America.

For the creation of the visualizer, only respondents who participated in at least three waves of the study were considered, resulting in a total of three thousand six hundred sixty-six individuals.

Measurement

  • We developed a measurement framework for social cohesion in Chile based on the approach proposed by Chan et al. (2006).

  • The framework is based on two dimensions:

    • Horizontal: addresses the relationships between individuals and social groups

    • Vertical: captures the interactions between individuals and social institutions

The measurement with social cohesion in VISELSOC is quite similar to VISLATAM as both make the distinction between horizontal and vertical dimensions proposed by Chan et al in (2006)

But the main difference is that with ELSOC, we have more items for analyzing social cohesion than VISLATAM, and we could divelop a more comprehensive measurement framework for Chile

In the case of horizontal cohesion, we can see that there are three subdimensions: public safety, territorial ties, and social networks.

Public safety consists of objective safety —such as the frequency of exposure to violent situations— and subjective safety, which refers to perceptions of safety.

Territorial ties measures people’s sense of belonging to their neighborhood and residents’ satisfaction with their local area.

Social networks are composed of three subdimensions: prosocial behavior, financial assistance, and interpersonal trust.

For vertical cohesion, we have four subdimensions. First, Trust in institutions, which measures trust in various political institutions such as the government, the political parties, and the Congress

Second, political attitudes and participation which includes political self-efficacy, political interest and political participation

Then, we have preferences for authoritarianism, which captures the extent to which individuals support authoritarian values and practices.

Finally, we have distributive justice that is composed by how unfair people consider the distribution of healthcare, education, and pensions to be

Measurement

  • We applied a several analytical techniques for to arrive at the final version of the measurement framework.

  • Sub-dimensions were calculated as average indices to facilitate the interpretation of the results.

  • All the decisions behind the construction of the framework can be found in this methodological document.

We applied a several analytical tekníks for to arrive at the final version of the measurement framework such as descriptive analyses, correlation matrices, both exploratory and confirmatory factor analysis to verify that the model’s underlying structure was optimal for its application in the Chilean context.

Although we confirmed the factor structure of the data, all sub-dimensions were calculated as average indices to facilitate the interpretation of the results

Results VISELSOC

Now I would like to show you what we can do with VISELSOC. So, the visualizer has three types of analyses.

Longitudinal

First, we have the longitudinal section, where we can observe the subdimensions and how they change over time.

So let’s focus on trust on political institutions

To understand the plot, the x-axis shows the years as categories, and the “wai”-axis shows the number of people who rated the highest categories—that is, 4 and 5—in terms of percentage

Now, using the data provided by the graph, we can see that trust in institutions fluctuates over time, reaching its lowest point in 2019. This can be explained by the social crisis Chile experienced that year, a contentious period known as the “social uprising.”

Ranking

In the ranking section, we can select a sub-dimension and compare the indicators that make it up, so let’s continue with trust in political institutions

First, we can see that at the right bottom there are the legends to every item. So, we can see the exact percentage when we hover over a chart.

In this case, trust in the government is an indicator that has change drastically over time, reaching a high of 40% and a low of 16% the following year. On the other hand, trust in political parties is at a historic low, having peaked just 14% at 2018

Bivariate

And finally we have the bivariate section, where we can plot the available subdimensions, but with the added benefit of being able to observe differences by category.

So let’s cross trust in political institutions with education

These were just a couple of examples of what we can do with VISELSOC, and we think that it could be interesting for you in case you would like to make something similar with your panel data.
